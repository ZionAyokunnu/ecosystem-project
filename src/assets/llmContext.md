
# Fundamental Context for Socio-Economic Ecosystem Analysis
*Version: 2.0 | Updated: January 2025*

## Analytical Voice & Purpose
LLM outputs should provide not just analysis, but directional insight. Each prompt response should reflect:
- **Business mode**: Strategic foresight, efficiency-minded recommendations, system optimization.
- **Community mode**: Empowerment-driven insight, social cohesion, inclusion, and wellbeing.

Across both, the tone should reflect that of a **consultant, coach, mentor, activist, and pastoral leader**—guiding with hope, clarity, and urgency.

Avoid passive commentary. Prioritize:
- Clear recommendations  
- Systemic understanding  
- Recognition of complexity  
- Motivating tone that affirms agency and promotes direction

## Core Indicators Definitions

## Wellbeing (Primary Outcome)
	•	Definition: Well-being goes beyond material or economic prosperity to include psychological, social, environmental, and spiritual aspects of life. It is multidimensional, dynamic, and shaped by interactions across personal, societal, and systemic levels.
	•	Range: 0–100 (higher indicates greater well-being)
	•	Key Components:
	•	Health: Includes physical, mental, and social health dimensions. Strongly linked to well-being via metrics like Healthy Lifetime Income (Zhang et al., 2023) and WHO indices.
	•	Interest & Love: Emotional engagement with life and interpersonal relationships. Interest drives lifelong motivation (Ainley, 2012; Fenouillet et al., 2017), while love fosters emotional resilience (Esch & Stefano, 2005).
	•	Critical Thinking: Associated with stronger psychological and emotional outcomes, especially among youth (Anaconas et al., 2024; Ding, 2024).
	•	Altruism & Philanthropy: Boosts individual and collective well-being through giving, volunteering, and community participation (Geng et al., 2022; Tashjian et al., 2021).
	•	Stability & Security: Emotional and physical safety, economic resilience, and community support (Lucchesi et al., 2021; Sano et al., 2021).
	•	Rights & Justice Standards: Societal well-being is shaped by dignity, inclusion, and access to justice (Seaford, 2018; Dano, 2022).
	•	Purpose & Productivity: Includes personal meaning, life goals, and perceived usefulness. Linked to resilience and lower negative affect (Hill et al., 2021; Wahrendorf et al., 2006).
	•	Accomplishment & Satisfaction: Positive correlation with career or academic success, job satisfaction, and long-term achievement (Sara & Omid, 2015).
	•	Information Literacy: A critical skill for navigating complex systems. Influences psychological and financial well-being, especially in vulnerable or rural populations (Kuutol et al., 2024; Li & Chen, 2023).
	•	Data Collection:
	•	OECD Better Life Index (11 dimensions including housing, education, and health)
	•	UN Human Development Index (life expectancy, education, income)
	•	Gallup-Sharecare Well-Being Index (purpose, social, financial, community, physical)
	•	Social Progress Index (non-economic indicators such as safety and rights)
	•	WHO Well-being Index
	•	Local community surveys (e.g., life satisfaction, sense of purpose, perceived security)
	•	Business Impact:
	•	Impacts workforce mental health, productivity, and talent retention
	•	High well-being scores correlate with consumer confidence and economic resilience
	•	Influences leadership decisions, social license to operate, and ESG performance


Energy
	•	Definition
Energy security ensures households and institutions can function effectively, fostering safety, predictability, and resilience. Inadequate access creates vulnerabilities—particularly in rural and underserved areas—leading to economic stagnation and compromised quality of life. Clean energy systems further reduce indoor air pollution, a leading cause of respiratory disease, and stable electricity supplies enhance educational performance and productivity.
	•	Range
From severe energy insecurity (no or highly intermittent access) to full, reliable, clean energy supply.
	•	Key Components
	•	Access & Reliability: Percentage of population with continuous electricity
	•	Clean Energy Adoption: Use of renewables instead of biomass/fossil fuels
	•	Indoor Air Quality: Levels of pollutants from household fuel use
	•	Educational & Economic Enablement: Correlation of supply stability with school performance and productivity
	•	Data Collection
	•	National and household energy‐access surveys (e.g., grid connectivity, hours of supply)
	•	Energy‐provider records on generation/meter readings
	•	Air‐quality monitoring (indoor PM₂.₅/PM₁₀ measurements)
	•	Educational statistics on school performance (exam pass rates, attendance)
	•	Frequency: typically annually or semi‐annually
	•	Business Impact
	•	Reliable power boosts manufacturing output, reduces downtime, and lowers operational costs
	•	Clean energy investment spurs new industries (solar, wind) and cuts healthcare expenses linked to pollution
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Infrastructure policies targeting rural electrification and renewable incentives
	•	Regulations and subsidies to transition from biomass to clean cooking fuels



Waste
	•	Definition
Poor waste management drives disease, pollution, and unsafe living conditions. Improper disposal of solid, industrial, and hazardous waste contaminates air, soil, and water, exacerbating respiratory, gastrointestinal, and chronic illnesses. Communities with organized waste practices report lower morbidity and mortality rates.
	•	Range
From unregulated, open dumping to fully integrated, safe waste‐collection and disposal systems.
	•	Key Components
	•	Types of Waste: Solid municipal, industrial, hazardous
	•	Collection & Disposal Practices: Segregation, transport, treatment, landfill management
	•	Environmental Contamination: Measures of air, soil, and water pollutants
	•	Public Health Outcomes: Incidence of waste‐related diseases
	•	Data Collection
	•	Municipal records on waste volumes and recycling rates
	•	Environmental monitoring of leachate, particulate emissions, water‐quality sampling
	•	Health‐surveillance data on disease incidence in at‐risk areas
	•	Frequency: continuous environmental monitoring; quarterly/annual health and waste audits
	•	Business Impact
	•	Elevated healthcare costs and lost worker productivity in polluted areas
	•	Opportunities for recycling and waste‐to‐energy industries
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Regulations on hazardous waste disposal, e-waste recycling mandates
	•	Public campaigns and community‐led clean‐up initiatives



Environment Brand Equity
	•	Definition
The perceived value of a region’s environmental quality, natural resources, and sustainability efforts. High environmental brand equity fosters pride, trust, and collective responsibility, driving stewardship and enhancing physical and mental health. Clean air, green spaces, and biodiversity correlate with reduced stress, better well-being, and longevity.
	•	Range
From “environmentally degraded” (low equity) to “model sustainable region” (high equity).
	•	Key Components
	•	Air & Water Quality: Pollutant concentrations, ecosystem health
	•	Green Space & Biodiversity: Area of parks, species diversity indices
	•	Sustainability Initiatives: Renewable projects, pollution‐reduction campaigns
	•	Community Engagement: Participation in environmental programs (e.g., reforestation)
	•	Data Collection
	•	Public surveys on environmental perception and satisfaction
	•	Ecological assessments (biodiversity counts, habitat mapping)
	•	Pollution and resource‐use monitoring (annual emissions, water‐use data)
	•	Frequency: annual environmental audits; periodic citizen‐perception surveys
	•	Business Impact
	•	Attracts eco‐tourism and “green” investors
	•	Supports premium branding for local products (e.g., “clean‐air grown”)
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Guides land‐use planning, conservation policies, and green‐infrastructure funding



Food and Water
	•	Definition
Access to nutritious food and clean water underpins health, individual purpose, and societal stability. Scarcity drives political unrest, migration, and social fragmentation, while adequate nourishment boosts energy, cognition, and productivity.
	•	Range
From acute insecurity (famine/drought conditions) to robust food and water security.
	•	Key Components
	•	Availability & Accessibility: Per-capita food and water supply
	•	Quality & Safety: Nutritional content, contaminant levels in water
	•	Resilience & Stability: Variability due to climate/drought
	•	Nutrition & Health Outcomes: Rates of malnutrition, water-borne illness incidence
	•	Data Collection
	•	Food‐security surveys (e.g., FAO’s Food Insecurity Experience Scale)
	•	Water‐quality testing for pathogens and chemicals
	•	Agricultural‐production and irrigation‐usage statistics
	•	Frequency: annual to multi‐annual assessments; continuous quality monitoring
	•	Business Impact
	•	Drives agricultural markets and commodity pricing
	•	Impacts labor productivity and public‐health expenditures
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Informs water‐infrastructure investment and food‐assistance programs
	•	Shapes drought‐management and food‐subsidy policies

	•	Range
From limited access/low literacy to widespread, high‐quality educational attainment.
	•	Key Components
	•	Educational attainment levels
	•	Information literacy and critical‐thinking abilities
	•	Access to learning resources and technology
	•	Lifelong‐learning and skill‐development opportunities
	•	Data Collection
	•	National education‐attainment statistics (enrollment, graduation rates)
	•	Literacy and standardized skill‐assessment surveys
	•	Technology‐access metrics (e.g., internet penetration)
	•	Frequency: census/annual educational reports; periodic skill audits
	•	Business Impact
	•	Higher workforce productivity, innovation, and competitiveness
	•	Catalyzes knowledge‐economy sectors (R&D, tech industries)
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Drives equitable‐education policies and digital‐divide initiatives
	•	Encourages philanthropic support for schools and libraries

Health and Well-Being
	•	Definition
A multidimensional health profile—encompassing life expectancy, chronic‐disease prevalence, and mental well-being—that anchors core well‐being indicators like rights fulfillment, productivity, and accomplishment.
	•	Range
From poor public‐health metrics (low life expectancy, high disease burden) to excellent (long lives, low prevalence of chronic/mental illness).
	•	Key Components
	•	Physical health: life expectancy, chronic‐disease rates
	•	Mental health: psychological‐wellness scores, stress levels
	•	Healthcare access and equity
	•	Data Collection
	•	Vital‐statistics registries and hospital‐record databases
	•	National health surveys (e.g., WHO STEPS, mental‐health modules)
	•	Frequency: continuous health‐system reporting; annual surveys
	•	Business Impact
	•	Healthy populations drive higher labor‐force participation and reduced absenteeism
	•	Lower public and private healthcare expenditures
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Informs universal‐healthcare and mental‐health‐program policies
	•	Shapes preventive‐care and chronic‐disease‐management initiatives

Motivation and Spirit
	•	Definition
The intrinsic and extrinsic motivational forces and psychological resilience that propel personal achievement, societal contribution, and emotional well-being.
	•	Range
From low drive/resilience to high intrinsic motivation and communal spirit.
	•	Key Components
	•	Intrinsic vs. extrinsic motivation levels
	•	Psychological resilience and stress‐coping capacity
	•	Sense of accomplishment, interest, and love in activities
	•	Altruism, philanthropy, and collective engagement
	•	Data Collection
	•	Psychometric assessments (motivation and resilience scales)
	•	Surveys on life satisfaction and sense of purpose
	•	Participation rates in volunteer and community programs
	•	Frequency: periodic national/organizational well-being surveys
	•	Business Impact
	•	Higher employee engagement, lower turnover, greater innovation
	•	Reduced costs related to stress and burnout
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Supports policies fostering equitable opportunity, mental‐health resources, and volunteerism


Social (Inter-Human) Dimension → Well-Being

Cultural Dimension
	•	Definition
Collective histories, beliefs, and practices that forge identity, shared values, and moral frameworks—fueling emotional fulfillment, social cohesion, and altruism.
	•	Range
From fragmented or eroded cultural engagement to vibrant, deeply rooted cultural vitality.
	•	Key Components
	•	Engagement in arts, music, literature, and heritage preservation
	•	Shared moral and ethical traditions
	•	Cultural participation rates and diversity of expression
	•	Data Collection
	•	Surveys on attendance at cultural events and self‐reported cultural engagement
	•	Counts of cultural‐heritage sites and program offerings
	•	Qualitative studies on value transmission and identity
	•	Frequency: annual cultural‐sector reports; periodic social‐cohesion studies
	•	Business Impact
	•	Bolsters creative industries, tourism, and local branding
	•	Stimulates demand for cultural goods and services
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Underpins cultural‐preservation and arts‐funding policies
	•	Fosters intergenerational and intergroup solidarity

Community Dimension
	•	Definition
The strength of local relationships and networks that grant identity, belonging, and mutual support—shaping health, stability, accomplishment, and civic engagement.
	•	Range
From low‐cohesion, high‐isolation neighborhoods to highly integrated, supportive communities.
	•	Key Components
	•	Community participation and volunteerism rates
	•	Access to local healthcare and social services
	•	Perceptions of safety and trust (stability/security)
	•	Educational and cultural programs at the community level
	•	Local justice, equity, and philanthropic activities
	•	Data Collection
	•	Community surveys (sense of belonging, trust indices)
	•	Crime and safety statistics
	•	Facility‐density mapping (clinics, community centers)
	•	Frequency: regular social‐capital barometers; annual municipal reports
	•	Business Impact
	•	Stable communities attract investment and support local economic growth
	•	Lower crime and social‐service costs
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Guides local‐development and governance policies
	•	Informs funding for community centers, public‐safety, and health outreach programs

    Schools Dimension
	•	Definition
The quality, accessibility, and equity of formal education systems, as reflected in students’ sense of accomplishment, purposefulness and productivity, and the upholding of education as a fundamental right.
	•	Range
From minimal or inequitable schooling (low access, poor quality) to universal, high-quality, inclusive education systems.
	•	Key Components
	•	Academic Achievement: Standardized test scores, graduation rates
	•	Skill Development: Vocational training, critical-thinking curricula
	•	Extracurricular Engagement: Participation in arts, sports, clubs
	•	Equity & Inclusivity: Enrollment gaps by gender/income/ethnicity, special-needs support
	•	Data Collection
	•	Ministry/District records (enrollment, completion, student–teacher ratios)
	•	Standardized assessments and school inspection reports
	•	Surveys of student engagement and well-being
	•	Frequency: annual to biennial
	•	Business Impact
	•	Produces a more skilled workforce, driving innovation and productivity
	•	Lowers recruitment/training costs by matching graduate skills to industry needs
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Informs funding formulas, equity-driven allocation of resources
	•	Guides inclusive-education policies and anti-discrimination mandates



Family Dimension
	•	Definition
The stability, health, and rights environment within the family unit, encompassing parental support, absence of abuse/separation, and the activation of altruism and social support when families face distress.
	•	Range
From high-stress, abusive, or fragmented family settings to stable, nurturing, rights-respecting households.
	•	Key Components
	•	Health & Well-Being: Incidence of domestic abuse, mental-health metrics among family members
	•	Stability & Security: Rates of family separation/divorce, economic support, housing stability
	•	Rights Protection: Legal enforcement of child/adult protection laws, access to family services
	•	Altruism & Philanthropy: Community interventions, social-service referrals, aid programs
	•	Data Collection
	•	Social-services case records, police/justice statistics on domestic violence
	•	National health surveys (stress, depression rates in households)
	•	Child-welfare agency reports on family support utilization
	•	Frequency: continuous administrative data; annual survey updates
	•	Business Impact
	•	Stable family environments correlate with higher workforce participation and lower healthcare costs
	•	Demand for family-support services and related industries (therapy, childcare)
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Drives legislation on child protection, domestic-violence prevention, family leave policies
	•	Shapes funding for counseling, shelters, and parental-support programs



Systems, Governance & Orderliness
	•	Definition
The effectiveness, transparency, and ethical grounding of institutions, legal frameworks, and public-sector systems in ensuring stability, security, and the uncompromising right standard.
	•	Range
From weak, corrupt, or opaque governance structures to robust, accountable, rights-respecting institutions.
	•	Key Components
	•	Rule of Law & Accountability: Courts’ independence, anti-corruption indices
	•	Security & Public Order: Crime rates, policing efficacy, emergency-response capacity
	•	Economic Governance: Regulatory quality, investor-confidence measures
	•	Ethical Enforcement: Access to justice, protection of marginalized groups
	•	Data Collection
	•	World Bank/Governance Indicators, Transparency International CPI
	•	National crime and judicial-system statistics
	•	Public-opinion surveys on trust in institutions
	•	Frequency: annual to biennial
	•	Business Impact
	•	Predictable regulation fosters investment, reduces compliance uncertainty
	•	Stable security environments lower costs for insurance and risk mitigation
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Informs judicial reforms, anti-corruption campaigns, community-policing initiatives
	•	Guides transparency and open-government policies



Relationships Dimension
	•	Definition
The strength, quality, and diversity of interpersonal connections—familial, platonic, and romantic—and their influence on physical and mental health, interest & love, and altruistic behavior.
	•	Range
From social isolation and weak ties to rich, supportive networks of diverse relationships.
	•	Key Components
	•	Social Support: Frequency of contact with friends/family, support-network indices
	•	Emotional Bonds: Measures of love and intimacy, relationship satisfaction scores
	•	Health Outcomes: Morbidity and mortality differentials by social-connectedness
	•	Altruism & Reciprocity: Acts of kindness, volunteerism within one’s network
	•	Data Collection
	•	Longitudinal social-network surveys (e.g., Holt-Lunstad cohorts)
	•	Public-health cohort studies linking relationship metrics to outcomes
	•	Time-use diaries capturing social interaction frequency
	•	Frequency: major surveys every 2–5 years; continuous panel studies
	•	Business Impact
	•	Social‐capital‐rich communities support local enterprises and collaborative innovation
	•	Lower healthcare and absenteeism costs where strong networks buffer stress
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Shapes community-building programs, mental-health outreach, and anti‐loneliness initiatives
	•	Guides funding for social clubs, community centers, and peer‐support networks



Influence Dimension
	•	Definition
The capacity of individuals or organizations to inspire and mobilize others toward shared goals, while adhering to ethical standards and promoting altruism.
	•	Range
From minimal or coercive influence to ethically grounded, servant-leadership models.
	•	Key Components
	•	Leadership Reach & Resonance: Followers’ engagement levels, mentorship outcomes
	•	Ethical Standards: Transparency, fairness, adherence to human-rights principles
	•	Purpose Alignment: Extent to which influence is perceived as meaningful and goal-oriented
	•	Altruistic Outcomes: Philanthropic campaigns initiated, volunteer mobilization metrics
	•	Data Collection
	•	360-degree leadership assessments, organizational-culture surveys
	•	Nonprofit and campaign reports on volunteer turnout and fundraising
	•	Ethics and compliance audits
	•	Frequency: annual organizational reviews; campaign-cycle reporting
	•	Business Impact
	•	Strong ethical leadership enhances brand reputation, employee retention, and innovation
	•	Effective influence drives market adoption and stakeholder alignment
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Informs leadership-development programs, ethics training, and public-service leadership roles
	•	Guides regulation of lobbying, political-finance transparency



Religion Dimension
	•	Definition
The role of faith, communal worship, and spiritual values in fostering emotional support (interest & love), upholding ethical standards, and motivating altruistic service.
	•	Range
From weak or exclusionary religious engagement to vibrant, inclusive faith communities.
	•	Key Components
	•	Spiritual Engagement: Worship attendance, prayer/meditation frequency
	•	Ethical Teachings: Sermons/catechism promoting justice, human dignity, rights
	•	Community Service: Faith-based charity volumes, volunteer hours
	•	Emotional Well-Being: Self-reported life-satisfaction among congregants
	•	Data Collection
	•	National surveys on religious affiliation and practice (e.g., Pew, Gallup)
	•	Records from faith-based organizations on charitable activity
	•	Well-being questionnaires tied to religious participation
	•	Frequency: annual to multi-annual religious-lands surveys
	•	Business Impact
	•	Faith-based nonprofits are significant service providers in education, health, and social welfare
	•	Corporate philanthropy often channels through religious networks
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Guides interfaith dialogue programs, religious-freedom legislation, and faith-based social services



Art & Craft Dimension
	•	Definition
Participation in creative and traditional arts and crafts, which enhance psychological resilience (health indices), foster cultural continuity, and drive philanthropic/social-advocacy projects.
	•	Range
From negligible engagement to widespread access to arts education and cultural heritage preservation.
	•	Key Components
	•	Therapeutic Engagement: Art-therapy program enrollment, self‐expression measures
	•	Cultural Continuity: Number of active artisan cooperatives, heritage-craft apprenticeships
	•	Economic Empowerment: Income generated by craft cooperatives, market access for artisans
	•	Advocacy & Philanthropy: Art events raising funds for social causes
	•	Data Collection
	•	Cultural-sector surveys on participation and economic output
	•	Nonprofit reports on art-therapy outcomes and funding raised
	•	Artisan cooperative financial statements
	•	Frequency: annual cultural-economy reports; program evaluations per cycle
	•	Business Impact
	•	Creative industries contribute to GDP, tourism, and export revenues (e.g., handicrafts)
	•	Corporate sponsorship of arts boosts brand goodwill and community relations
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Informs arts-education funding, cultural-heritage grants, and creative-sector incentives



Events Dimension
	•	Definition
Organized gatherings (cultural, sporting, civic) that foster shared accomplishment, altruism (through fund-raising/volunteerism), and social cohesion.
	•	Range
From infrequent, poorly attended events to regular, large-scale, inclusive gatherings.
	•	Key Components
	•	Participation & Reach: Attendance figures, demographic diversity
	•	Emotional & Purpose Impact: Surveys of participant fulfillment and sense of achievement
	•	Philanthropic Outcomes: Funds raised, volunteer mobilization levels
	•	Social Cohesion: Measures of trust and intergroup interaction post-event
	•	Data Collection
	•	Event ticketing and registration platforms
	•	Post-event surveys on well-being impact
	•	Charity financial disclosures for fund-raising events
	•	Frequency: per event cycle (annual, quarterly, etc.)
	•	Business Impact
	•	Drives local economies via tourism, hospitality, and retail
	•	Sponsorships generate marketing ROI and brand engagement
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Shapes public-space planning, event-permit regulations, and grant support for festivals



Media Dimension
	•	Definition
The ecosystem of information channels (traditional and digital) and their influence on information literacy, critical thinking, civic engagement, and trust in institutions.
	•	Range
From unregulated, low-credibility media environments to diverse, well-fact–checked, media-literate ecosystems.
	•	Key Components
	•	Information Literacy: Audience ability to discern credible from false information
	•	Critical Thinking: Engagement metrics in analytical news consumption
	•	Civic Participation: Correlation of media exposure with voter turnout and public-forum involvement
	•	Institutional Trust: Public-opinion indices on media and government credibility
	•	Data Collection
	•	Media-consumption surveys (e.g., frequency and sources)
	•	Fact-checking organization reports on misinformation prevalence
	•	Civic-engagement statistics (e.g., election participation by demographic segment)
	•	Frequency: continuous media-analytics; biennial literacy assessments
	•	Business Impact
	•	Shapes advertising markets, subscription revenues, and platform viability
	•	Impacts brand reputation management in the face of misinformation
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Informs media-regulation policies, digital-literacy curricula, and public-broadcast funding



GDP per Capita Dimension
	•	Definition
Average economic output per person, indicating overall economic stability, living standards, and capacity to invest in social infrastructure.
	•	Range
From low GDP per capita (limited resources for public goods) to high GDP per capita (robust social spending capacity).
	•	Key Components
	•	Total GDP / Population: National accounts data
	•	Growth Trends: Year-on-year percentage changes
	•	Distributional Context: Gini-adjusted or median income comparisons
	•	Data Collection
	•	National statistical offices’ GDP estimates and census population counts
	•	International financial organizations’ harmonized data (IMF, World Bank)
	•	Frequency: quarterly GDP releases; annual recalibrations
	•	Business Impact
	•	High-GDP economies attract foreign direct investment and support high-value industries
	•	Consumer purchasing power fuels domestic markets
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Guides fiscal policy, social-welfare spending, and infrastructure investment decisions



Financial Security & Stability
	•	Definition
The degree to which individuals and households have reliable income, savings, and access to credit, reducing economic stress and enabling pursuit of personal and social goals.
	•	Range
From high financial vulnerability (little savings, unstable income) to strong security (diversified income, emergency reserves).
	•	Key Components
	•	Income Volatility: Frequency and magnitude of income shocks
	•	Savings & Debt Levels: Household debt ratios, savings-to-income metrics
	•	Credit Access: Rate of unbanked/underbanked populations, credit-score distributions
	•	Financial Literacy: Capability to budget, invest, and plan
	•	Data Collection
	•	Household finance surveys (e.g., HFCS, SCF)
	•	Banking and credit-bureau statistics
	•	National labor-market reports on job stability/unemployment
	•	Frequency: annual surveys; continuous financial-sector reporting
	•	Business Impact
	•	Financially secure customers are more likely to borrow responsibly and invest, expanding financial-services markets
	•	Employers benefit from lower turnover and absenteeism when employees are financially stable
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Underpins social-protection policies, minimum-wage legislation, and financial-inclusion initiatives



Economic Dimension
	•	Definition
The broader health of the economy—employment levels, income distribution, and growth stability—and its role in supporting individual and collective well-being.
	•	Range
From recessionary, high-unemployment settings to full-employment, low-inequality growth environments.
	•	Key Components
	•	Employment & Unemployment Rates
	•	Income Inequality: Gini coefficient, poverty-headcount ratio
	•	GDP Growth Stability: Volatility indices, business-cycle measures
	•	Data Collection
	•	Labor-force surveys, tax records, national accounts
	•	Household-income and expenditure surveys
	•	Frequency: monthly unemployment; quarterly GDP and inequality updates
	•	Business Impact
	•	Stable economies reduce credit-risk premiums and support capital formation
	•	Equitable growth expands consumer markets and social cohesion
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Guides labor-market reforms, progressive-tax policies, and anti-poverty programs

	•	Range
From underdeveloped, unreliable networks to resilient, high-coverage smart-infrastructure systems.
	•	Key Components
	•	Energy & Utilities: Grid reliability, broadband penetration
	•	Transport Networks: Urban design, public-transport coverage
	•	Environmental Defenses: Flood control, waste management
	•	Digital Inclusion: Rural/urban internet access gaps
	•	Data Collection
	•	National infrastructure audits, utility-provider performance metrics
	•	GIS mapping of transport and broadband coverage
	•	Climate-risk and resilience assessments
	•	Frequency: annual infrastructure reports; ongoing digital-coverage monitoring
	•	Business Impact
	•	Reliable infrastructure reduces logistics costs, spurs digital-economy growth
	•	Enables just-in-time supply chains and remote-work capabilities
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Informs capital-investment budgets, public–private partnership frameworks
	•	Shapes digital-inclusion and green-infrastructure policies



Roads & Access Dimension

The reach, quality, and safety of road and pedestrian networks, influencing emergency-response times, economic connectivity, and perceptions of security.
	•	Range
From sparse, poorly maintained paths to dense, well-lit, multi-modal transport corridors.
	•	Key Components
	•	Network Density & Condition: Kilometers of paved roads per capita, maintenance backlog

	•	Accessibility: Proportion of population within X minutes of all-weather roads
	•	Data Collection
	•	Transport-ministry infrastructure inventories
	•	Police and health data on road-traffic incidents
	•	GIS-based access-time modeling
	•	Frequency: continuous maintenance logs; annual safety audits
	•	Business Impact
	•	Lowers distribution costs, expands labor-market catchment areas
	•	Improves supply-chain resilience and just-in-time delivery
	•	Community Impact / Policy Relevance
	•	Guides road-safety campaigns, maintenance-funding allocations, and rural-access programs



Tools Availability

Access to specialized equipment, technologies, and resources that enable individuals and organizations to maintain livelihoods, innovate, and respond to challenges.
	•	Range
From severe tool/resource scarcity to widespread, affordable access to advanced technologies and equipment.
